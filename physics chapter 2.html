What is Mechanics?
  • Mechanics → branch of physics that deals with motion and the forces that cause it
  • Explains how objects move and what factors affect that motion:
    • Force
    • Mass
    • Momentum
    • Energy

Branches of Mechanics

Based on Concept of Motion

  • Dynamics → studies motion and its causes (forces)
  • Kinematics → describes motion without considering causes (no forces)

Based on System Studied

  • Particle Mechanics → object so small that shape and internal structure do not affect the problem
  • System of Particles → two or more particles interacting with each other or with external forces

Where is Mechanics Applied?

  • Celestial Mechanics → motions of stars, planets, satellites
    • These motions can be predicted with high accuracy thousands of years in advance
  • Everyday Objects → cars, bridges, aircraft, blood flow, etc.
    • Newton's laws work well here because objects move at speeds much less than the speed of light
  • Atomic and Subatomic Scale → atoms, molecules, radiation
    • Classical mechanics fails → quantum mechanics is needed

Kinematics in One and Two Dimensions
Describing Motion Without Forces

What is Kinematics?

  • Term "Kinematics" comes from Greek word "kinesis" → meaning motion
  • Related words:
    • Cinema → moving pictures
    • Kinesiology → study of human motion
  • Definition: Kinematics is the branch of mechanics that describes motion without considering its causes (no forces involved)

What Does Kinematics Study?

  • Kinematics focuses on analyzing kinematical quantities, such as:
    • Displacement → change in position
    • Velocity → rate of change of position
    • Acceleration → rate of change of velocity
    • Time → duration of motion
    • Trajectory → path of motion

Examples of Motion Around Us

  • Planets orbiting the Sun
  • Cars moving along a road
  • Blood flowing through veins
  • Athletes running on a track
Why Start Physics with Kinematics?
Before understanding why objects move (Dynamics), we must first describe how they move.

Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration in 1D and 2D
Kinematical Quantities

Position (r)

  • Position → tells us where an object is located relative to a reference point (origin)
  • Represented by:
    • In 1D → \(x\) or \(y\) coordinate
    • In 2D → position vector \(\vec{r} = (x, y)\)
  • Note: Position is always measured relative to a reference frame
Example: A car parked 5 m to the right of a traffic light (origin) → position = \(+5 \, \text{m}\) on the x-axis

Displacement (\(\Delta x\) or \(\Delta \vec{r}\))

  • Displacement → change in position of an object during motion
  • Vector quantity → magnitude and direction
  • Depends only on initial and final positions, not on the path taken
  • Formulas:
    • 1D motion: \(\Delta x = x_f - x_i\)
    • 2D motion: \(\Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i\)
  • Properties:
    • Displacement can be positive, negative, or zero
    • If object returns to starting point → \(\Delta x = 0\) (even if it traveled a long distance)
Example: A ball moves from \(x_i = 2 \, \text{m}\) to \(x_f = 10 \, \text{m}\):
\(\Delta x = 10 - 2 = 8 \, \text{m}\) to the right

Distance (S)

  • Distance → total length of the path traveled by the object
  • Scalar quantity (magnitude only, no direction)
Example: Running around a 400 m track and returning to start:
• Distance = 400 m
• Displacement = 0 m
Key Difference:
• Distance → measures how much ground was covered
• Displacement → measures how far final position is from initial position (straight line)
Average and Instantaneous Velocity

Average Velocity (\(v_{av}\))

  • Average velocity → overall rate of displacement during a time interval
  • Formula: \(v_{av} = \dfrac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{\vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i}{t_f - t_i}\)
  • Vector quantity → magnitude and direction (same direction as displacement)

Average Speed

  • Formula: \(\text{speed}_{av} = \dfrac{S}{\Delta t}\) (total distance divided by total time)
  • Scalar quantity
Key point: Average speed is always \(\geq\) magnitude of average velocity because distance \(\geq\) displacement.

Instantaneous Velocity (\(\vec{v}\))

  • Instantaneous velocity → velocity at a specific moment in time
  • Rate of change of position at that instant
  • Instantaneous velocity is the limiting value of the ratio \(\frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}\) as \(\Delta t\) approaches zero
\(\vec{v} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t}\)
  • Expression using derivative: \(v = \dfrac{d\vec{r}}{dt}\) (derivative of position with respect to time)
  • Graphical meaning: slope of the position-time graph at a given point
  • Instantaneous speed: magnitude of the instantaneous velocity

Instantaneous Speed

  • The magnitude of instantaneous velocity
  • Always positive
Average and Instantaneous Acceleration

Average Acceleration (\(a_{av}\))

  • Average acceleration → rate of change of velocity over a time interval
  • Formula: \(a_{av} = \dfrac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t_f - t_i}\)
  • Vector quantity → magnitude and direction
  • If velocity increases → acceleration is positive
  • If velocity decreases → acceleration is negative (deceleration or retardation)

Instantaneous Acceleration (\(a\))

  • Instantaneous acceleration → acceleration at a specific instant of time
  • Instantaneous acceleration is the limiting value of the ratio \(\frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}\) as \(\Delta t\) approaches zero
\(\vec{a} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}\)
  • Expression using derivative: \(a = \dfrac{d\vec{v}}{dt}\) (rate of change of velocity with respect to time)
  • Graphical meaning: slope of the velocity-time graph at a given point

Exercise
A person walks first at a constant speed of 5 m/s along a straight line from point A to point B, and then back along the same line from B to A at a constant speed of 3 m/s.
  • a) What is his average speed over the entire trip?
  • b) What is his average velocity over the entire trip?
Solution (a):
\(v_{av} = \dfrac{\text{total distance }(S)}{\text{total time interval }(\Delta t)}\)

Total distance covered = \(S_{AB} + S_{BA} = 2x\)

\(v_{av} = \dfrac{S_{tot}}{t_{tot}} = \dfrac{S_{AB} + S_{BA}}{t_{AB} + t_{BA}}\)

\(t_{AB} = \dfrac{x}{v_{AB}} = \dfrac{x}{5 \, \text{m/s}}\)

\(t_{BA} = \dfrac{x}{v_{BA}} = \dfrac{x}{3 \, \text{m/s}}\)

\(v_{av} = \dfrac{2x}{\left(\dfrac{x}{5} + \dfrac{x}{3}\right)} = 2x \left( \dfrac{15}{8x} \right) = \dfrac{15}{4} = 3.75 \, \text{m/s}\) Solution (b):
Average velocity over the entire trip = zero

\(\vec{r}_f = \vec{r}_i \quad \rightarrow \quad \Delta \vec{r} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i = 0\)

\(\vec{v}_{av} = \dfrac{\Delta \vec{r}}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{\vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i}{t_f - t_i} = 0\)

Motion with Constant Acceleration
  • When an object moves with constant acceleration → velocity changes at a constant rate over time
  • This allows us to derive simple, widely-used equations for both 1D and 2D motion

Velocity and Acceleration

  • For constant acceleration: \(a = \dfrac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{v_f - v_i}{t}\) (assuming initial time \(t_i = 0\))
  • Rearranging, we get the first equation of motion:
\(v_f = v_i + a t\)
  • Where:
    • \(v_i\) = initial velocity
    • \(v_f\) = final velocity
    • \(a\) = constant acceleration
    • \(t\) = time interval

Average Velocity and Displacement

  • Average velocity under constant acceleration:
\(v_{avg} = \dfrac{v_i + v_f}{2}\)
  • Displacement: \(\Delta r = v_{avg} \times t\)
  • Substitute \(v_{avg}\): \(\Delta r = \dfrac{v_i + v_f}{2} \times t\)
  • Using \(v_f = v_i + a t\), we get the second equation of motion:
\(\Delta r = v_i t + \dfrac{1}{2} a t^2\)

Derivation of \(v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2a\Delta r\)

  • Step 1: Start from the first two equations:
    • \(v_f = v_i + a t\)
    • \(\Delta r = v_i t + \frac{1}{2} a t^2\)
  • Step 2: Solve the first equation for \(t\): \(t = \dfrac{v_f - v_i}{a}\)
  • Step 3: Substitute \(t\) into displacement equation:
\(\Delta r = v_i \left(\dfrac{v_f - v_i}{a}\right) + \dfrac{1}{2} a \left(\dfrac{v_f - v_i}{a}\right)^2\)
  • Step 4: Simplify the first term: \(\dfrac{v_i(v_f - v_i)}{a}\)
  • Step 5: Simplify the second term: \(\dfrac{1}{2} \cdot \dfrac{(v_f - v_i)^2}{a}\)
  • Step 6: Combine both terms and factor:
\(\Delta r = \dfrac{(v_f - v_i)(v_f + v_i)}{2a}\)
  • Step 7: Multiply both sides by \(2a\):
\(2a\Delta r = (v_f - v_i)(v_f + v_i) = v_f^2 - v_i^2\)
  • Step 8: Therefore:
\(v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2a\Delta r\)

Equations for 2D Motion

  • For motion in two dimensions:
    • Acceleration vector: \(\vec{a} = a_x \hat{i} + a_y \hat{j}\)
    • Initial velocity: \(\vec{v}_i = v_{x,i} \hat{i} + v_{y,i} \hat{j}\)
    • Final velocity: \(\vec{v}_f = v_{x,f} \hat{i} + v_{y,f} \hat{j}\)
  • Velocity components:
\(v_{x,f} = v_{x,i} + a_x t\)
\(v_{y,f} = v_{y,i} + a_y t\)
  • Displacement components:
\(\Delta x = v_{x,i} t + \dfrac{1}{2} a_x t^2\)
\(\Delta y = v_{y,i} t + \dfrac{1}{2} a_y t^2\)
  • Total displacement: \(\Delta r = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}\)
Tip: Treat each component as independent 1D motion.

Summary of Key Equations

  • \(v_f = v_i + a t\)
  • \(\Delta r = v_i t + \dfrac{1}{2} a t^2\)
  • \(v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2a\Delta r\)
  • \(v_{avg} = \dfrac{v_i + v_f}{2}\)
  • 2D motion → apply formulas separately to x and y components

Examples
Example 1: A truck covers 40 m in 8.5 s while smoothly slowing down to a final speed of 2.8 m/s. Find:
a) Its original speed
b) Its acceleration

Solution:
Given: \(S = 40 \, \text{m}\), \(t = 8.5 \, \text{s}\), \(v_f = 2.8 \, \text{m/s}\)

Average velocity: \(v_{av} = \dfrac{v_f + v_i}{2}\)

Displacement: \(S = \left(\dfrac{v_f + v_i}{2}\right) t \quad \rightarrow \quad v_i = \dfrac{2S}{t} - v_f\)

a) \(v_i = \dfrac{2(40 \, \text{m})}{8.5 \, \text{s}} - 2.8 \, \text{m/s} = 9.41 \, \text{m/s} - 2.8 \, \text{m/s} = 6.61 \, \text{m/s}\)

b) \(a = \dfrac{v_f - v_i}{t} = \dfrac{2.8 \, \text{m/s} - 6.61 \, \text{m/s}}{8.5 \, \text{s}} = -0.448 \, \text{m/s}^2\) (deceleration)

Example 2: A jet plane lands with a speed of 100 m/s and slows down at a rate of 5 m/s² as it comes to rest.
a) What is the time interval needed by the jet to come to rest?
b) Can this jet land on an airport where the runway is 0.8 km long?

Solution:
Given: \(v_i = 100 \, \text{m/s}\), \(a = -5 \, \text{m/s}^2\), \(v_f = 0\)

a) \(a = \dfrac{v_f - v_i}{t} \quad \rightarrow \quad t = \dfrac{v_f - v_i}{a} = \dfrac{0 - 100}{-5} = 20 \, \text{s}\)

b) Distance required to stop: \(S = \left(\dfrac{v_f + v_i}{2}\right) t = \left(\dfrac{0 + 100}{2}\right) \times 20 = 1000 \, \text{m} = 1 \, \text{km}\)

The jet cannot land because the runway (0.8 km) is shorter than the distance required (1 km).

Free Fall Motion
Motion Under Gravity

What is Free Fall?

  • Free fall → motion of an object near the surface of the Earth when the only force acting on it is gravity
  • If we ignore air resistance → all objects fall with the same constant acceleration called acceleration due to gravity (g)

Key Facts about g

  • On Earth's surface, the standard value is \(g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\)
  • \(g\) is not exactly constant everywhere:
    • Greater at the poles than at the equator
    • Greater at sea level than at the top of a mountain
    • Can slightly vary due to local geology
  • For most practical purposes (up to about 16 km altitude) → take \(g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\)
Example: A girl throws a ball upwards, giving it an initial speed \(u = 15 \, \text{m/s}\). Neglect air resistance.
(a) How long does the ball take to return to the girl's hand?
(b) What will be its velocity then?
Solution (a):
Choose positive y upward with origin at the girl's hand → \(y_i = 0\)
Acceleration is negative (downward): \(a = -g = -9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\)

When ball returns to hand → \(y = 0\)
Using \(y - y_i = ut - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\):
\(0 = (15 \, \text{m/s})t - \frac{1}{2}(9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2)t^2\)
\(0 = t \left(15 - \frac{1}{2} \times 9.8 \times t\right)\)
\(t = \dfrac{2 \times 15}{9.8} = 3.1 \, \text{s}\)
Solution (b):
Using \(v^2 = u^2 - 2g(y - y_i)\):
Since \(y = y_i = 0\):
\(v^2 = u^2 = (15)^2 = 225\)
\(v = \pm 15 \, \text{m/s}\)

Select negative sign because the ball is moving downward just before returning to the girl's hand → \(v = -15 \, \text{m/s}\)

Projectile Motion
Motion of a Projectile

What is Projectile Motion?

  • Projectile → any object thrown obliquely into space that follows a path determined by gravitational force acting on it
  • Examples:
    • Stone projected at an angle
    • Bomb released from an airplane
    • Shot fired from a gun
    • Shot put or javelin thrown by an athlete
Figure: Motion of a projectile – trajectory, velocity components, maximum height (h), and range (R)
[projectile_diagram.png]
  • Velocity of Projection (u) → velocity with which the body is projected
  • Angle of Projection (θ) → angle between direction of projection and horizontal plane
  • Trajectory (OAB) → path described by projectile from point of projection to where it reaches the horizontal plane → trajectory is a parabola

Basic Assumptions in Projectile Motion

  • Free fall acceleration \(g\) is constant over the range of motion and directed downward
  • Effect of air resistance is negligible
  • With these assumptions → path of projectile is a downward parabola

Components of Motion

[projectile_components.png]
  • \(u_x = u \cos \theta\) → component of velocity along x-axis
  • \(u_y = u \sin \theta\) → component of velocity along y-axis
  • \(a_x = 0\) → no force acting horizontally
  • \(a_y = -g\) → acceleration due to gravity (downward)

Horizontal Position (x_f)

  • Using equation: \(\Delta x = u_x t + \frac{1}{2} a_x t^2\)
  • Assuming projectile starts at origin \((x_i, y_i) = (0, 0)\)
  • Since \(a_x = 0\) and \(u_x = u \cos \theta\):
\(x_f = u \cos \theta \cdot t + \frac{1}{2}(0)t^2 = u \cos \theta \cdot t\)

Vertical Position (y_f)

  • Using equation: \(\Delta y = u_y t + \frac{1}{2} a_y t^2\)
  • \(u_y = u \sin \theta\) and \(a_y = -g\):
\(y_f = u \sin \theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2\)

Horizontal Velocity (v_x)

  • Using equation: \(v_x = u_x + a_x t\)
  • Since \(a_x = 0\) and \(u_x = u \cos \theta\):
\(v_x = u \cos \theta\) (constant throughout motion)

Vertical Velocity (v_y)

  • Using equation: \(v_y = u_y + a_y t\)
  • \(u_y = u \sin \theta\) and \(a_y = -g\):
\(v_y = u \sin \theta - g t\)

Maximum Height (h)

  • At maximum height → vertical velocity becomes zero: \(v_y = 0\)
  • \(0 = u \sin \theta - g t\) → \(t = \dfrac{u \sin \theta}{g}\) (time to reach maximum height)
  • Substitute this time into vertical position equation:
\(h = u \sin \theta \left(\dfrac{u \sin \theta}{g}\right) - \dfrac{1}{2} g \left(\dfrac{u \sin \theta}{g}\right)^2\)
\(h = \dfrac{u^2 \sin^2 \theta}{g} - \dfrac{1}{2} \dfrac{u^2 \sin^2 \theta}{g} = \dfrac{u^2 \sin^2 \theta}{2g}\)

Time of Flight (T)

  • Total time of flight = twice the time to reach maximum height (symmetrical motion)
\(T = 2 \times \dfrac{u \sin \theta}{g} = \dfrac{2u \sin \theta}{g}\)

Horizontal Range (R)

  • Range = horizontal velocity × total time of flight
\(R = (u \cos \theta) \times \left(\dfrac{2u \sin \theta}{g}\right) = \dfrac{2u^2 \sin \theta \cos \theta}{g}\)
  • Using trigonometric identity: \(2 \sin \theta \cos \theta = \sin 2\theta\)
\(R = \dfrac{u^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}\)
  • Range is maximum when \(\sin 2\theta = 1\) → \(2\theta = 90^\circ\) → \(\theta = 45^\circ\)
\(R_{\text{max}} = \dfrac{u^2}{g}\)

Summary

Quantity Formula Explanation
Horizontal displacement \(x = (u \cos \theta) t\) Increases linearly with time
Vertical displacement \(y = (u \sin \theta) t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2\) Parabolic path
Horizontal velocity \(v_x = u \cos \theta\) Constant
Vertical velocity \(v_y = u \sin \theta - g t\) Changes linearly with time
Maximum height (h) \(h = \dfrac{u^2 \sin^2 \theta}{2g}\) Where \(v_y = 0\)
Time of flight (T) \(T = \dfrac{2u \sin \theta}{g}\) Symmetric rise and fall
Range (R) \(R = \dfrac{u^2 \sin 2\theta}{g}\) Maximum at \(\theta = 45^\circ\)

Projectile Motion Examples
Example 1: A rocket is fired with an initial velocity of 100 m/s at an angle of 55° above the horizontal. It explodes on the mountain side 12 s after its firing. What are the x- and y-coordinates of the rocket relative to its firing point?

Solution:
Given: \(u = 100 \, \text{m/s}\), \(\theta = 55^\circ\), \(t = 12 \, \text{s}\)

Horizontal position (x):
\(x(t) = u \cos \theta \cdot t\)
\(x = (100 \, \text{m/s}) \times \cos 55^\circ \times (12 \, \text{s})\)
\(\cos 55^\circ = 0.5736\)
\(x = 100 \times 0.5736 \times 12\)
\(x = 688.3 \, \text{m}\)

Vertical position (y):
\(y(t) = u \sin \theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2\)
\(\sin 55^\circ = 0.8192\)
\(y = (100 \times 0.8192 \times 12) - \frac{1}{2} \times 9.8 \times (12)^2\)
\(y = 983.0 - \frac{1}{2} \times 9.8 \times 144\)
\(y = 983.0 - 705.6\)
\(y = 277.4 \, \text{m}\)

Answer: The rocket is at \((688.3 \, \text{m}, 277.4 \, \text{m})\) relative to its firing point.

Example 2: A plane drops a package to a party of explorers. If the plane is traveling horizontally at 40 m/s and is 100 m above the ground, where does the package strike the ground relative to the point at which it is released?


Solution:
Given: \(u = u_x = 40 \, \text{m/s}\), \(\theta = 0^\circ\), vertical displacement \(y = -100 \, \text{m}\) (below the reference point)

Step 1: Find the time of flight using the vertical motion equation
\(y(t) = u \sin \theta \cdot t - \frac{1}{2} g t^2\)
Since \(\theta = 0^\circ\), \(\sin 0^\circ = 0\):
\(y(t) = 0 - \frac{1}{2} g t^2\)
\(-100 = -\frac{1}{2} \times 9.8 \times t^2\)
\(-100 = -4.9 \, t^2\)
\(t^2 = \frac{100}{4.9} = 20.41\)
\(t = \sqrt{20.41} = 4.52 \, \text{s}\)

Step 2: Find the horizontal displacement
\(x(t) = u \cos \theta \cdot t = u_x \cdot t\) (since \(\cos 0^\circ = 1\))
\(x = 40 \, \text{m/s} \times 4.52 \, \text{s}\)
\(x = 180.8 \, \text{m}\)

Answer: The package strikes the ground 181 m from the release point.

Particle Dynamics and Planetary Motion
What Causes Motion?
  • In kinematics → studied motion using displacement, velocity, and acceleration
  • We did not ask: what causes motion or changes in motion?
  • Now we focus on:
    • What makes objects remain at rest or accelerate?
    • What keeps planets in motion around the Sun?
  • Answer: Mass of the object and the forces acting on it

Why Newton's Laws Matter

  • Explain most motions in everyday life
  • Analyze static structures (bridges, buildings)
  • Study planetary motion → Newton developed his laws to explain Kepler's laws

Force

  • Force → any interaction that can change the motion of an object
  • A force can:
    • Start or stop motion
    • Change speed or direction of motion
    • Change the shape or size of an object

Net Force (Fnet)

  • Net force → vector sum of all forces acting on an object
  • An object accelerates only if the net force ≠ 0
  • If Fnet = 0 → object is either at rest or moving with constant velocity (Newton's 1st Law)

Concept of Force as a Measure of Interaction
  • All forces come from four fundamental interactions:
    • Gravitational Force
    • Electromagnetic Force
    • Strong Nuclear Force
    • Weak Nuclear Force

Each Fundamental Force is Described By

  • The type of particles that feel the force
  • Its relative strength
  • Its range (short or infinite)
  • The kind of particles that carry (mediate) the force
Key Insight: Every force in nature (friction, tension, normal force) is just a result of these fundamental forces.

Types of Forces

Contact Forces

    Require physical contact
  • Muscular Force
  • Frictional Force
  • Normal Force
  • Applied Force
  • Tension Force
  • Spring Force
  • Air Resistance (Drag Force)

Non-Contact Forces

    Act at a distance
  • Gravitational Force
  • Magnetic Force
  • Electrostatic Force

Contact Forces

  • Muscular Force → produced by muscles; exists only when in contact with an object → breathing, lifting, pushing, pulling
  • Frictional Force → opposes motion between two surfaces in contact; acts opposite to direction of motion → lighting a matchstick, stopping a moving ball
  • Normal Force → perpendicular force exerted by a surface on an object resting on it → book on a table
  • Applied Force → force applied by a person or object to another object → pushing a table
  • Tension Force → force in a stretched rope, string, or cable; pulls equally at both ends → pulling a bucket with a rope
  • Spring Force → force exerted by a stretched or compressed spring; can be push or pull → trampoline
  • Air Resistance (Drag Force) → frictional force acting on objects moving through air; resists motion → parachute slowing a skydiver

Non-Contact Forces

  • Gravitational Force → attractive force between any two masses → \(F \propto \dfrac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\) → water drops falling, planets orbiting the Sun
  • Magnetic Force → force exerted by magnets on magnetic materials or other magnets → magnet attracting an iron nail
  • Electrostatic Force → force between electrically charged bodies; attractive (opposite charges) or repulsive (like charges) → rubbed balloon sticking to a wall

Newton's Laws of Motion
Foundations of Classical Mechanics
  • First formulated by Sir Isaac Newton in 1687
  • Developed to explain why planets move in elliptical orbits instead of perfect circles
  • Limitations: Does not fully apply to very tiny particles (electrons) or objects moving near the speed of light

1. Newton's First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

  • Statement: An object will remain at rest, or move with constant velocity in a straight line, unless acted upon by an external force
  • Inertia → tendency of an object to resist any change in its motion
  • Object at rest will not move until a force acts on it
  • Object in motion keeps moving in the same direction and speed unless something stops or changes it
  • Examples: Book on a table stays still until pushed; car on highway would keep going forever without friction and air resistance
  • No net force = constant velocity: If forces balance out (net force = 0) → object at rest stays at rest, object in motion continues at constant velocity

2. Newton's Second Law of Motion

  • Statement: The acceleration acquired by a point particle is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. The acceleration is always in the direction of the net force.
  • Direct proportionality: greater net force → greater acceleration
  • Inverse proportionality: greater mass → less acceleration for the same force
\(\sum F = m \times a\)
\(\sum F\) → net force (N), \(m\) → mass (kg), \(a\) → acceleration (m/s²)
  • When net force = 0 → acceleration = 0 → matches First Law
Example 1: A box with mass 40 kg is at rest on a frictionless floor. You apply a 20 N force horizontally. What is the acceleration?

Mass \(m = 40 \, \text{kg}\)

Forces of Friction
Resisting Relative Motion
  • Friction → contact force that resists the relative motion (or tendency of motion) between two surfaces in contact
  • When two objects touch → a frictional force develops at their interface
  • This force depends mainly on:
    • Surface texture (smooth vs rough)
    • Normal force (N) → force pressing the two surfaces together
  • Without friction → walking, driving, or even holding objects would be impossible

Dependence of Friction on Normal Force

  • Normal force (N) → force perpendicular to the surface
  • Friction is always proportional to normal force:
\(F_{\text{frict}} \propto F_{\text{normal}}\)
\(F_{\text{frict}} = \mu F_{\text{normal}}\)
  • \(\mu\) → coefficient of friction (no unit)
  • Depends only on the nature of surfaces, not on the contact area

Types of Friction

  • Static Friction (\(f_s\)):
    • Acts when object is at rest
    • Prevents motion until a threshold force is applied
    • Formula: \(f_s = f_{s,\text{max}} = \mu_s N\)
    • Example: pushing a heavy box that resists moving at first
  • Kinetic Friction (\(f_k\)):
    • Acts when object is sliding
    • Formula: \(f_k = \mu_k N\)
    • Always \(\mu_k < \mu_s\) → easier to keep an object moving than to start moving it
    • Typical \(\mu\) values: 0.03 (ice on ice) to 1.0 (rubber on concrete)

Direction of Friction

  • Friction force always acts parallel to the surface
  • Opposes:
    • Motion (if object is sliding)
    • Tendency of motion (if object is stationary)

Uniform Circular Motion
Motion in a circular path with constant speed
  • Uniform Circular Motion → motion of objects in a circular path with a constant speed
  • Objects moving in a circular path with a constant speed can have acceleration
\(\vec{a} = \dfrac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}\)
  • Acceleration can occur due to:
    • Change in magnitude of the velocity
    • Change in direction of the velocity
  • For uniform circular motion → acceleration arises because of the change in direction of the velocity

Properties of Uniform Circular Motion

  • Velocity is always tangent to the circular path and perpendicular to the radius
  • Acceleration is always perpendicular to the circular path and points toward the center of the circle
  • Such acceleration is called centripetal acceleration
\(|\vec{r}_1| = |\vec{r}_f| = r\)

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation
The force that governs falling bodies and planetary motion
  • Gravity is the weakest of the four basic forces found in nature
  • Newton was the first scientist to precisely define the gravitational force and show it could explain both falling bodies and astronomical motions
  • Newton was the first to propose an exact mathematical form and show that the motion of heavenly bodies should be conic sections → circles, ellipses, parabolas, and hyperbolas

Newton's Universal Law of Gravitation

  • The gravitational force is always attractive
  • It depends only on the masses involved and the distance between them
  • Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force along a line joining them
  • The force is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them
  • Gravitational attraction is along a line joining the centers of mass of the two bodies
  • The magnitude of the force is the same on each → consistent with Newton's third law
Figure: Interaction of two objects with gravitational force
[gravitational_force.png]
\(\vec{F} = \dfrac{GmM}{r^2} \hat{r}\)
  • \(r\) → distance between the centers of mass of the bodies
  • \(G\) → gravitational constant → \(G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{Nm}^2/\text{kg}^2\) in SI units
Key insight: The small magnitude of the gravitational force is consistent with everyday experience. We are unaware that even large objects like mountains exert gravitational forces on us. Our body weight is the force of attraction of the entire Earth on us with a mass of \(6 \times 10^{24} \, \text{kg}\).

Acceleration Due to Gravity (g)

  • The weight of an object \(mg\) is the gravitational force between it and Earth
  • Substituting \(mg\) for \(F\) in Newton's universal law of gravitation:
\(mg = \dfrac{GmM}{r^2}\)
  • Where:
    • \(m\) → mass of the object
    • \(M\) → mass of Earth
    • \(r\) → distance to the center of Earth
  • The mass \(m\) of the object cancels, leaving:
\(g = \dfrac{GM}{r^2}\)
Substituting known values:
\(g = (6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{Nm}^2/\text{kg}^2) \left( \dfrac{5.98 \times 10^{24} \, \text{kg}}{(6.38 \times 10^6 \, \text{m})^2} \right)\)

\(g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2\)
  • The distance between the centers of mass of Earth and an object on its surface is very nearly the same as the radius of Earth
  • The value of \(g\) is independent of the body's mass
  • Newton's law explains Galileo's observation that all masses fall with the same acceleration in terms of a universally existing force of attraction between masses

Kepler's Laws, Satellite Motion and Weightlessness
Foundations of planetary motion
  • The basic laws of planetary motion were established by Johannes Kepler (1571-1630) based on analysis of astronomical observations of Tycho Brahe (1546–1601)
  • In 1609, Kepler formulated the first two laws → third law was discovered in 1619
  • Later, in the late 17th century, Isaac Newton proved mathematically that all three laws of Kepler are a consequence of the law of universal gravitation

Kepler's First Law (Law of Orbits)

  • States that the orbit of each planet in the solar system is an ellipse, with the Sun at one focus
  • The points \(F_1\) and \(F_2\) are known as the foci of the ellipse
  • The closer together these points are → the more closely the ellipse resembles a circle
  • A circle is the special case of an ellipse where the two foci are at the same location
  • All planets orbit the Sun in a path that resembles an ellipse, with the Sun located at one of the foci of that ellipse
[kepler_first_law.png]

Kepler's Second Law (Law of Areas)

  • States that "the radius vector connecting the centers of the Sun and the Planet sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time"
  • The second law describes the speed (constantly changing) at which any given planet moves while orbiting the Sun
  • A planet moves fastest when it is closest to the Sun (perihelion)
  • A planet moves slowest when it is furthest from the Sun (aphelion)
  • If an imaginary line were drawn from the center of the planet to the center of the Sun → that line would sweep out the same area in equal periods of time
[kepler_second_law.png]
Key insight: Kepler's laws describe how planets move. Newton's law of universal gravitation later explained why they move that way.

Kepler's Third Law (Law of Harmony)

  • States that "the square of the orbital period of a planet is proportional to the cube of the average distance between the centers of the planet and the Sun"
  • Unlike Kepler's first and second laws that describe motion of a single planet → the third law makes a comparison between different planets
  • The ratio of the squares of the periods to the cubes of their average distances from the Sun is the same for every planet
\(T^2 \propto R^3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \dfrac{T^2}{R^3} = \text{constant}\)

Mathematical Formulation

  • \(T\) → orbital period (time for one complete revolution)
  • \(R\) → average distance from the Sun (orbital radius)
  • The constant is the same for all planets orbiting the Sun
Key insight: Amazingly, every planet has the same \(T^2/R^3\) ratio. This law allowed astronomers to predict orbital periods and distances for newly discovered planets.

  • Jumping off a high dive before hitting the water

Work and Energy
Definition of work and the mass-spring system
  • In physics, work is more specific than everyday usage → work is the displacement of an object due to force
  • Work → transfer of energy due to an applied force
  • Work is done on an object when energy is transferred to that object
  • The energy of a moving object is called kinetic energy

Essential Conditions for Work

  • Force must be exerted on the object
  • The force must cause a motion or displacement
  • The force should have a component along the line of displacement

Work Done by a Constant Force

  • For a particle subjected to a constant force \(\vec{F}\) undergoing displacement \(\Delta \vec{r}\):
\(W = \vec{F} \cdot \Delta \vec{r} = |\vec{F}| |\Delta \vec{r}| \cos \theta\)
  • \(\theta\) → angle between \(\vec{F}\) and \(\Delta \vec{r}\)
  • Work is a scalar quantity
  • SI unit of work is Joule (J) → \(1 \, \text{J} = 1 \, \text{N} \cdot 1 \, \text{m}\)
  • The sign of work depends on the direction of \(\vec{F}\) relative to \(\Delta \vec{r}\)
  • Work done by the applied force is positive when the projection of \(\vec{F}\) onto \(\Delta \vec{r}\) is in the same direction as \(\Delta \vec{r}\)

Mass-Spring System (Hooke's Law)

  • A block on a horizontal, frictionless surface connected to a spring
  • If the spring is stretched or compressed from equilibrium → it exerts a force:
\(F_s = -kx \quad \text{(Hooke's Law)}\)
  • \(x\) → position of the block relative to its equilibrium (\(x = 0\)) position
  • \(k\) → force constant or spring constant (positive constant)
  • \(F_s\) → restoring force
  • The negative sign signifies that the force exerted by the spring is always directed opposite to the displacement from equilibrium
[mass_spring_system.png]

Work Done by the Restoring Force

  • Work done if the block undergoes displacement from \(x_i\) to \(x_f\) is the area under the force-displacement graph
  • Work = area enclosed by the graph:
\(W = \dfrac{1}{2} (F_s \times \Delta X), \quad \text{where} \quad F_s = -k \Delta X\)
\(W = \dfrac{1}{2} k x_i^2 - \dfrac{1}{2} k x_f^2\)

Work Done by the Applied Force

  • For the applied force (\(F_{app} = k \Delta X\)):
\(W_{app} = \dfrac{1}{2} k x_f^2 - \dfrac{1}{2} k x_i^2\)
Key insight: The work done by the restoring force is the negative of the work done by the applied force. The expression \(\frac{1}{2}kx^2\) represents the elastic potential energy stored in the spring.

Example: A particle is subject to a force \(F_x\) that varies with position as shown in the figure below. What is the total work done by the force over the distance from \(x = 0\) to \(x = 15.0 \, \text{m}\)?

  • The total work done is equal to the area under the \(F_x\) versus \(x\) curve

Energy

  • Energy is the crown of physics → found in every branch of physics
  • Energy → capacity of a physical system to perform work
  • Exists in several forms → kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical, and other forms
  • SI unit of energy is Joule (J)

Kinetic Energy (KE)

  • Kinetic energy → capacity of an object to do work by virtue of its motion
  • For an object of mass \(m\) moving with speed \(v\):
\(KE = \dfrac{1}{2} mv^2\)

Potential Energy (PE)

  • Potential energy → energy that is stored in an object due to its position relative to some zero position
  • An object possesses gravitational potential energy if it is positioned at a height above (or below) the zero height
  • The potential energy concept can be used only when dealing with a special class of forces called conservative forces

Gravitational Potential Energy

  • Mathematically, the change in gravitational potential energy is given by:
\(\Delta U_g = mg \Delta y\)
  • Where:
    • \(m\) → mass of the object
    • \(g\) → acceleration due to gravity
    • \(\Delta y\) → change in height
  • Note: This equation is valid only for objects near the surface of the Earth, where \(g\) is approximately constant
Key insight: Kinetic energy is energy of motion, while potential energy is energy of position. The sum of both forms is called mechanical energy.

Work-Energy Theorem
The relationship between work and kinetic energy
  • One possible outcome of doing work on a system is that the system changes its speed
  • We should consider the work done by all forces acting on a particle (net work) to see its effect on the particle's motion

Derivation of the Work-Energy Theorem

  • Let a force \(F\) be applied on an object initially moving with velocity \(u\)
  • If it is displaced by a displacement \(s\) and changes its velocity to \(v\):
\(v^2 - u^2 = 2as\)
  • Multiplying by \(m\) and dividing by 2:
\(\dfrac{mv^2}{2} - \dfrac{mu^2}{2} = mas\)
  • Since \(F = ma\):
\(\dfrac{mv^2}{2} - \dfrac{mu^2}{2} = Fs\)
  • Where \(W = Fs\) is the work done by the force:
\(\dfrac{mv^2}{2} - \dfrac{mu^2}{2} = W\)
  • Therefore:
\(\dfrac{1}{2}mv^2 - \dfrac{1}{2}mu^2 = W\)
Key result: The work done is equal to the change in kinetic energy of the object.
\(W_{\text{net}} = \Delta KE\)
  • The work-kinetic energy theorem states: In the case where work is done on a system and the only change in the system is in its speed

Example

A 0.600-kg particle has a speed of 2.00 m/s at point A and kinetic energy of 7.50 J at point B. What is (a) its kinetic energy at A? (b) its speed at B? (c) the total work done on the particle as it moves from A to B?

Solution

  • Given: \(m = 0.6 \, \text{kg}\), \(v_A = 2 \, \text{m/s}\), \(K_B = 7.5 \, \text{J}\)
(a) Kinetic energy at A:
\(K_A = \dfrac{1}{2} m v_A^2\)
\(K_A = \dfrac{1}{2} (0.6 \, \text{kg}) (2 \, \text{m/s})^2\)
\(K_A = \dfrac{1}{2} (0.6)(4) = 1.2 \, \text{J}\)
(b) Speed at B:
\(K_B = \dfrac{1}{2} m v_B^2\)
\(v_B = \sqrt{\dfrac{2K_B}{m}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{2(7.5 \, \text{J})}{0.6 \, \text{kg}}}\)
\(v_B = \sqrt{\dfrac{15}{0.6}} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \, \text{m/s}\)
(c) Total work done from A to B:
From work-kinetic energy theorem:
\(W_{AB} = K_B - K_A = 7.5 \, \text{J} - 1.2 \, \text{J} = 6.3 \, \text{J}\)
Answers: (a) \(K_A = 1.2 \, \text{J}\), (b) \(v_B = 5 \, \text{m/s}\), (c) \(W_{AB} = 6.3 \, \text{J}\)

Mechanical Energy
The sum of kinetic and potential energy in an isolated system
  • Mechanical Energy (ME) → sum of kinetic energy and potential energy
\(ME = KE + PE\)

Derivation for a Falling Book

  • Consider the book-Earth system. As the book falls from \(y_b\) to \(y_a\):
\(W_{\text{on book}} = (-mg)(y_a - y_b) = mgy_b - mgy_a\)
  • From the work–kinetic energy theorem → work done on the book equals the change in kinetic energy:
\(W_{\text{on book}} = \Delta K_{\text{book}}\)
  • As the book falls from initial height \(y_b\) to final height \(y_a\):
\(mgy_b - mgy_a = -(mgy_a - mgy_b) = -(U_f - U_i) = -\Delta U_g\)
\(\Delta K_{\text{book}} = -\Delta U_g\)
\(\Delta K_{\text{book}} + \Delta U_g = 0\)
\((K_f - K_i) + (U_f - U_i) = 0\)
\(K_f + U_f = K_i + U_i\)
Conservation of Mechanical Energy: The mechanical energy of an isolated, friction-free system is conserved.
  • An isolated system → no energy transfers across the boundary
  • For an object of mass \(m\) falling in a gravitational field:
\(\dfrac{1}{2} mv_f^2 + mgy_f = \dfrac{1}{2} mv_i^2 + mgy_i\)
Key insight: When only conservative forces act, the total mechanical energy remains constant. Energy can transform between kinetic and potential forms, but the sum never changes.

Power
Time rate of energy transfer
  • Power → time rate of energy transfer
  • If an external force is applied to an object and the work done in time interval \(\Delta t\) is \(W\):
\(P_{av} = \dfrac{W}{\Delta t} \quad \text{(Average power)}\)
  • Instantaneous power → limiting value of average power as \(\Delta t \to 0\):
\(P = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \dfrac{W}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{dW}{dt}\)
  • Since \(W = \vec{F} \cdot \Delta \vec{r}\):
\(P = \dfrac{dW}{dt} = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}\)
  • In general, power is defined for any type of energy transfer:
\(P = \dfrac{E}{t}\)

Units of Power

  • SI unit → joules per second (J/s) → called the watt (W) (after James Watt):
\(1 \, \text{W} = 1 \, \text{J/s} = 1 \, \text{kg} \cdot \text{m}^2 / \text{s}^3\)
  • U.S. customary unit → horsepower (hp):
\(1 \, \text{hp} = 746 \, \text{W}\)

Kilowatt-Hour (kWh)

  • A unit of energy (or work) defined in terms of power:
  • One kilowatt-hour → energy transferred in 1 hour at constant rate of 1 kW = 1000 J/s
\(1 \, \text{kWh} = (10^3 \, \text{W})(3600 \, \text{s}) = 3.6 \times 10^6 \, \text{J}\)

Example

Example: An older model car accelerates from rest to speed \(v\) in 10 seconds. A newer, more powerful car accelerates from rest to \(2v\) in the same time period. What is the ratio of the power of the newer car to that of the older car?

Solution

\(P_{av} = \dfrac{W}{\Delta t}\)

\(W_{\text{old}} = \Delta K = \dfrac{1}{2} mv^2 - 0 = \dfrac{1}{2} mv^2\)

\(W_{\text{new}} = \Delta K = \dfrac{1}{2} m(2v)^2 - 0 = \dfrac{1}{2} m(4v^2) = 2mv^2\)

\(P_{\text{old}} = \dfrac{W_{\text{old}}}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{mv^2}{2(\Delta t)}\)

\(P_{\text{new}} = \dfrac{W_{\text{new}}}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{2mv^2}{\Delta t}\)

\(\dfrac{P_{\text{new}}}{P_{\text{old}}} = \dfrac{\dfrac{2mv^2}{\Delta t}}{\dfrac{mv^2}{2(\Delta t)}} = \dfrac{2mv^2}{\Delta t} \times \dfrac{2(\Delta t)}{mv^2} = 4\)
Answer: The ratio of the power of the newer car to that of the older car is 4:1.

Linear Momentum, Impulse, and Conservation of Momentum
The quantity of motion and its conservation in isolated systems
  • Momentum → quality of a moving object to exert a force on anything that tries to stop it
  • Linear momentum of a particle of mass \(m\) moving with velocity \(\vec{v}\):
\(\vec{P} = m\vec{v}\)
  • Momentum (\(\vec{P}\)) is a vector quantity in the direction of the velocity
  • SI unit of momentum → kg·m/s

Newton's Second Law in Terms of Momentum

  • Using Newton's second law, we can relate linear momentum to the net force:
\(\vec{F} = m \dfrac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{\Delta (m\vec{v})}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{\Delta \vec{P}}{\Delta t}\)
  • The time rate of change of linear momentum of a particle is equal to the net force acting on the particle
\(\Delta \vec{P} = \vec{F} \Delta t\)

Impulse

  • Impulse (\(\vec{I}\)) → the product of net force and the time interval during which it acts
\(\vec{I} = \Delta \vec{P} = \vec{P}_f - \vec{P}_i = \vec{F} \Delta t\)
Impulse-Momentum Theorem: The impulse of the net force acting on a particle is equal to the change in momentum of the particle.

Conservation of Linear Momentum

  • Recall Newton's third law → when two objects of masses \(m_1\) and \(m_2\) interact:
\(\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \vec{F}_{12} + \vec{F}_{21} = 0\)
\(m_1 \dfrac{\Delta \vec{v}_1}{\Delta t} + m_2 \dfrac{\Delta \vec{v}_2}{\Delta t} = 0\)
\(\Rightarrow m_1 \Delta \vec{v}_1 + m_2 \Delta \vec{v}_2 = 0\)
\(\Rightarrow m_1 \vec{v}_{1i} + m_2 \vec{v}_{2i} = m_1 \vec{v}_{1f} + m_2 \vec{v}_{2f}\)
\(\vec{P}_{1i} + \vec{P}_{2i} = \vec{P}_{1f} + \vec{P}_{2f}\)
Law of Conservation of Linear Momentum: Whenever two or more particles in an isolated system interact, the total momentum of the system remains constant.
  • This result can be extended to any number of particles in an isolated system
  • An isolated system → no external forces act on the system
\(\vec{P}_{\text{total, initial}} = \vec{P}_{\text{total, final}}\)
  • Applications of conservation of momentum:
    • Collisions between objects (cars, billiard balls, particles)
    • Rocket propulsion → exhaust gases moving backward propel rocket forward
    • Recoil of a gun when bullet is fired
Example: A 60-kg archer stands at rest on frictionless ice and fires a 0.50-kg arrow horizontally at 50 m/s. With what velocity does the archer move across the ice after firing the arrow?

Solution

Let \(m_1\) = mass of the archer = 60 kg
Let \(m_2\) = mass of the arrow = 0.50 kg
Consider the archer + arrow as an isolated system

\(\vec{P}_{\text{tot}} = \text{constant} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \vec{P}_i = \vec{P}_f\)

\(m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}\)

Initially, the archer + arrow is at rest → \(v_{1i} = v_{2i} = 0\)

\(0 = (60 \, \text{kg}) v_{1f} + (0.50 \, \text{kg})(50 \, \text{m/s})\)

\(0 = 60 v_{1f} + 25\)

\(60 v_{1f} = -25\)

\(v_{1f} = \dfrac{-25}{60} = -0.42 \, \text{m/s}\)

The negative sign indicates the archer moves opposite to the direction of the arrow.
This is an example of recoil similar to a gun firing a bullet.
Answer: The archer moves across the ice with a velocity of 0.42 m/s in the direction opposite to the arrow's motion.

Collisions
Elastic, inelastic, and perfectly inelastic collisions
  • Consider a collision between two particles of masses \(m_1\) and \(m_2\)
  • If the two particles form an isolated system, the total momentum is conserved
  • Total momentum just before collision = total momentum just after collision
  • Total kinetic energy may or may not be conserved → determines the type of collision

A. Elastic Collision

  • An elastic collision → both total kinetic energy and total momentum of the system are conserved
  • Perfectly elastic collisions occur between atomic and subatomic particles
  • For two particles moving along the same straight line:
\(m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}\)

\(\dfrac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2i}^2 = \dfrac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1f}^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2f}^2\)
  • From conservation of kinetic energy (canceling \(\frac{1}{2}\)):
\(m_1 (v_{1i}^2 - v_{1f}^2) = m_2 (v_{2f}^2 - v_{2i}^2)\)
  • Factoring both sides:
\(m_1 (v_{1i} - v_{1f})(v_{1i} + v_{1f}) = m_2 (v_{2f} - v_{2i})(v_{2f} + v_{2i})\)
  • From conservation of momentum:
\(m_1 (v_{1i} - v_{1f}) = m_2 (v_{2f} - v_{2i})\)
  • Dividing the factored kinetic energy equation by the momentum equation:
\(v_{1i} + v_{1f} = v_{2f} + v_{2i}\)
\(v_{1i} - v_{2i} = -(v_{1f} - v_{2f})\)
Relative velocity relation for elastic collisions: The relative velocity of approach before collision equals the negative of the relative velocity of separation after collision.

B. Inelastic Collision

  • An inelastic collision → total kinetic energy of the system is not conserved
  • Momentum of the system is conserved
\(m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}\)

\(\dfrac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2i}^2 \neq \dfrac{1}{2} m_1 v_{1f}^2 + \dfrac{1}{2} m_2 v_{2f}^2\)

C. Perfectly Inelastic Collision

  • When the colliding objects stick together after the collision → perfectly inelastic collision
  • Both objects move with a common velocity \(v_f\) after collision
\(m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = (m_1 + m_2) v_f\)
\(v_f = \dfrac{m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i}}{m_1 + m_2}\)
Example 1: An archer shoots an arrow toward a target that is sliding toward her with a speed of 2.50 m/s on a smooth surface. The 22.5-g arrow is shot with a speed of 35.0 m/s and passes through the 300-g target, which is stopped by the impact. What is the speed of the arrow after passing through the target?

Solution

Given: \(m_1 = 22.5 \, \text{g}\), \(m_2 = 300 \, \text{g}\), \(v_{1i} = 35 \, \text{m/s}\), \(v_{2i} = -2.5 \, \text{m/s}\) (negative because target moves toward archer), \(v_{2f} = 0\)

From conservation of linear momentum:

\(m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}\)

\((22.5)(35) + (300)(-2.5) = (22.5) v_{1f} + 0\)

\(787.5 - 750 = 22.5 v_{1f}\)

\(37.5 = 22.5 v_{1f}\)

\(v_{1f} = \dfrac{37.5}{22.5} = 1.67 \, \text{m/s}\)
Answer: The speed of the arrow after passing through the target is 1.67 m/s.

Example 2: A block of mass \(m_1 = 1.6 \, \text{kg}\) initially moving to the right with a speed of 4 m/s on a horizontal frictionless track collides with a second block of mass \(m_2 = 2.1 \, \text{kg}\) initially moving to the left with a speed of 2.5 m/s. If the collision is elastic, find the velocities of the two blocks after collision.

Solution

Given:
\(m_1 = 1.6 \, \text{kg}\), \(v_{1i} = 4 \, \text{m/s}\) (to the right, positive)
\(m_2 = 2.1 \, \text{kg}\), \(v_{2i} = -2.5 \, \text{m/s}\) (to the left, negative)
Collision is elastic → both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved.

From conservation of momentum:
\(m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}\)

\((1.6)(4) + (2.1)(-2.5) = 1.6 v_{1f} + 2.1 v_{2f}\)

\(6.4 - 5.25 = 1.6 v_{1f} + 2.1 v_{2f}\)

\(1.15 = 1.6 v_{1f} + 2.1 v_{2f}\) (equation 1)

From conservation of kinetic energy (elastic collision relation):
\(v_{1i} + v_{1f} = v_{2i} + v_{2f}\)

\(v_{1f} - v_{2f} = v_{2i} - v_{1i}\)

\(v_{1f} - v_{2f} = -2.5 - 4\)

\(v_{1f} - v_{2f} = -6.5\) (equation 2)

Solving equations (1) and (2) simultaneously:
From equation (2): \(v_{1f} = v_{2f} - 6.5\)

Substitute into equation (1):
\(1.6(v_{2f} - 6.5) + 2.1 v_{2f} = 1.15\)

\(1.6 v_{2f} - 10.4 + 2.1 v_{2f} = 1.15\)

\(3.7 v_{2f} = 11.55\)

\(v_{2f} = \dfrac{11.55}{3.7} = 3.12 \, \text{m/s}\)

\(v_{1f} = 3.12 - 6.5 = -3.38 \, \text{m/s}\)
Answer:
\(v_{1f} = -3.38 \, \text{m/s}\) (block 1 moves to the left)
\(v_{2f} = 3.12 \, \text{m/s}\) (block 2 moves to the right)
  • The negative sign for \(v_{1f}\) indicates block 1 reverses direction after collision
  • Block 2 continues moving to the right but with increased speed

Center of Mass
The point where all mass can be considered to be concentrated
  • Center of mass → point at which all the mass can be considered to be concentrated
  • The center of mass of a system is located on the line joining the particles and is closer to the particle having the larger mass

Center of Mass for Two Particles on the x-axis

\(x_{CM} = \dfrac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2}{m_1 + m_2}\)

Center of Mass for a System of Many Particles (3D)

  • For a system of \(n\) particles with masses \(m_i\):
\(x_{CM} = \dfrac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2 + m_3 x_3 + ... + m_n x_n}{m_1 + m_2 + m_3 + ... + m_n} = \dfrac{\sum_i m_i x_i}{M}\)
\(y_{CM} = \dfrac{\sum_i m_i y_i}{M} \quad \text{and} \quad z_{CM} = \dfrac{\sum_i m_i z_i}{M}\)
  • Where \(M = \sum_i m_i\) is the total mass of the system
  • In vector form:
\(\vec{r}_{CM} = x_{CM} \hat{i} + y_{CM} \hat{j} + z_{CM} \hat{k} = \dfrac{\sum_i m_i \vec{r}_i}{M}\)

Example 1

Example: A system consists of three particles with masses \(m_1 = 1.0 \, \text{kg}\), \(m_2 = 1.0 \, \text{kg}\), and \(m_3 = 2.0 \, \text{kg}\) located as shown in the figure below. Find the center of mass of the system.

Solution

x-coordinate of center of mass:
\(x_{CM} = \dfrac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2 + m_3 x_3}{m_1 + m_2 + m_3}\)

\(x_{CM} = \dfrac{(1 \, \text{kg})(1 \, \text{m}) + (1 \, \text{kg})(2 \, \text{m}) + (2 \, \text{kg})(0 \, \text{m})}{1 \, \text{kg} + 1 \, \text{kg} + 2 \, \text{kg}}\)

\(x_{CM} = \dfrac{1 + 2 + 0}{4} = \dfrac{3}{4} = 0.75 \, \text{m}\)
y-coordinate of center of mass:
\(y_{CM} = \dfrac{m_1 y_1 + m_2 y_2 + m_3 y_3}{m_1 + m_2 + m_3}\)

\(y_{CM} = \dfrac{(1 \, \text{kg})(0 \, \text{m}) + (1 \, \text{kg})(0 \, \text{m}) + (2 \, \text{kg})(2 \, \text{m})}{1 \, \text{kg} + 1 \, \text{kg} + 2 \, \text{kg}}\)

\(y_{CM} = \dfrac{0 + 0 + 4}{4} = 1 \, \text{m}\)
